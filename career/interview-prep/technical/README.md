# Optimizing Your Technical Interview

## Technical Interview Overview

After passing the phone screenings, you'll usually move on to the technical part of the interview process. This step may vary significantly from one company to another. While one company may have a more business-focused approach to their questions (for example, they might describe their product and ask you to come up with a cost-effective solution), other companies may have a more engineering-focused interview process. In these cases, you may be asked a few questions about best coding practices, algorithms, and even system design.

You might be asked to do a take-home assignment, such as designing and implementing a RESTful API, building a small full-stack application, or optimizing a database query for performance and then presenting it to a member of the company's technical team at an in-person interview. Or you could be invited for an in-person interview and asked some questions about the tech stack in the job description for the role, algorithms, data structures, and system design.

Since there's no standard process used by all companies when hiring software engineers, it's important to do your own research. Try to understand each company's business model and make educated guesses about the technical issues they may be facing. This will help you tailor your preparation process to the particular company you're applying to.

There's no single best way to prepare for a technical interview, but there are some strategies that can help:

Research the company and the team you're interviewing for.
If you're struggling with a specific question, ask for help and think aloud. Some questions will be intentionally open-ended — the interviewer wants to know your thought process.
When appropriate, allow yourself to change the interview direction towards the technical areas where you feel most comfortable.

## Take Home Assessments

Home Assignments: Code Challenges
If things went well with your HR interview you might receive a code challenge from the company within a few days after your first interview.

Coding challenges are done at home and closely resemble real coding situations. Typically, a company will give you a specific amount of time to complete and test a set task based on a product spec. Then there will be an interview where you discuss your code with an emphasis on why you chose a certain solution and how it addresses the requirements. You could even prepare a presentation to show your logic.

Coding challenges are usually more project-based and are less like the timed algorithm questions. A typical example for such a task would be: "Build a simplified task management app with user authentication and a REST API." or something along those lines. It shouldn't take more than a few hours of your time and they may give you from 1 to 7 days to do it.

What if something is not clear with the task?
When you receive the code challenge, it's extremely important to make sure that you understand all of the instructions. If something is unclear, do not hesitate to reach out to HR to clarify it.

After you submit a task, you'll get a response confirming that it's successfully been submitted and further information on when you can expect to receive feedback. You may be asked to send a short demo video or a slide deck guiding your potential employer through the solution you have built. If you are not asked to do so, consider making a demo or a slide deck to make your submission stand out.
