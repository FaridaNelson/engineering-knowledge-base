Tech Interview: Whiteboarding
There are several scenarios for live coding interviews. Depending on the job stack you will be asked to prove you are able to perform tasks within a particular area of software engineering. Let's take a closer look at the whiteboard interview situations.

The main idea is for the hiring team to observe your process, methodology, and reasoning. And there are a few classic ways in which this is done:

showing a system design or algorithms and data structure solutions via a whiteboard
coding for a timed technical assessment
System Design questions will focus on specific, fundamental points within a certain programming language. These are more common in smaller companies and can be a differentiator for candidates with non-traditional career backgrounds (e.g., those without a history of technical work).

What actually is "whiteboarding"?
During the interview, you may need to use a real whiteboard or draw.io (if the interview is conducted remotely). This is actually one of the most invaluable skills to have for a real job as well. Whiteboarding is so important that Google is actually infamous for making you do your code challenge on Google Docs, forcing you to create a solution without your IDE. We strongly suggest that you practice whiteboarding and are always prepared for it!

For example, you will be asked to design a feature, such as a messaging app. As you sketch out your solution, you'll be asked why you have chosen certain languages and methods. The interviewer may change the requirements and describe things inadequately. This is often done on purpose to prompt you to ask good questions, be adaptable, and address new features as they are required, just like in real life.

It’s all about asking questions, communicating the problem, and deriving a solution.

The interviewer will ask you some questions, point out some things, and largely just watch you.

Another very popular scenario includes timed technical assessment with a task such as, for example, creating a clone of the TripleTen website with a back-end database. You might alternatively be asked to perform a task on a platform such as HackerRank or Codility. Or there may be some broken code. You’ll need to identify the problem, fix it, write a test, pass it, and be able to explain why you did it that way. To help prepare for this kind of challenge, you should practice different types of tests as well as try coding outside of your comfort zone.

While any question can be asked in an interview, there are some questions that are more common than others. We suggest revisiting the following parts of the course: object prototypes, "this", JS tasks on whiteboard coding, Complexity Analysis of Algorithms and functions in-depth.
